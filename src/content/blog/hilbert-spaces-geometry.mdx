## Introduction

A **Hilbert space** is the natural setting for geometry in infinite dimensions. It retains the essential features of Euclidean space — angles, orthogonality, projections — while allowing for the infinite-dimensional function spaces that arise in quantum mechanics, signal processing, and partial differential equations. As John von Neumann recognized, Hilbert spaces are the mathematical framework for quantum theory.

## Definition and Examples

A **Hilbert space** $H$ is a complete inner product space. That is, $H$ is a vector space equipped with an inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ such that the induced norm $\|x\| = \sqrt{\langle x, x \rangle}$ makes $H$ a Banach space (complete normed space).

### Fundamental Examples

**1.** $\mathbb{R}^n$ with $\langle x, y \rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i$.

**2.** $\ell^2$ — the space of square-summable sequences:

$$
\ell^2 = \left\{(x_n)_{n=1}^\infty : \sum_{n=1}^\infty |x_n|^2 < \infty\right\}, \quad \langle x, y \rangle = \sum_{n=1}^\infty x_n \overline{y_n}
$$

**3.** $L^2([a,b])$ — the space of square-integrable functions:

$$
\langle f, g \rangle = \int_a^b f(x)\overline{g(x)}\,dx
$$

**4.** The **Sobolev space** $H^1([0,1]) = \{f \in L^2 : f' \in L^2\}$ with:

$$
\langle f, g \rangle_{H^1} = \int_0^1 f\overline{g}\,dx + \int_0^1 f'\overline{g'}\,dx
$$

## The Parallelogram Law

An inner product always satisfies the **parallelogram law**:

$$
\|x + y\|^2 + \|x - y\|^2 = 2\|x\|^2 + 2\|y\|^2
$$

**Theorem (von Neumann–Jordan).** A normed space is an inner product space if and only if the parallelogram law holds. The inner product is then recovered via the **polarization identity**:

$$
\langle x, y \rangle = \frac{1}{4}\left(\|x+y\|^2 - \|x-y\|^2\right)
$$

This explains why $L^p$ for $p \neq 2$ is not a Hilbert space — the parallelogram law fails.

## Orthogonality and Projections

Two elements $x, y \in H$ are **orthogonal** ($x \perp y$) if $\langle x, y \rangle = 0$.

**Projection Theorem.** Let $M$ be a closed subspace of $H$. For every $x \in H$, there exists a unique $m_0 \in M$ minimizing $\|x - m\|$ over $m \in M$. Moreover:

$$
H = M \oplus M^\perp
$$

where $M^\perp = \{y \in H : \langle y, m \rangle = 0 \text{ for all } m \in M\}$.

The **orthogonal projection** $P_M: H \to M$ satisfies:
- $P_M^2 = P_M$ (idempotent)
- $P_M^* = P_M$ (self-adjoint)
- $\|P_M\| = 1$ (if $M \neq \{0\}$)

## Orthonormal Bases

A set $\{e_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}$ is an **orthonormal system** if $\langle e_\alpha, e_\beta \rangle = \delta_{\alpha\beta}$. It is an **orthonormal basis** (complete orthonormal system) if its closed linear span equals $H$.

**Bessel's Inequality.** For any orthonormal system $\{e_n\}$ and $x \in H$:

$$
\sum_n |\langle x, e_n \rangle|^2 \leq \|x\|^2
$$

**Parseval's Identity.** If $\{e_n\}$ is an orthonormal basis:

$$
\|x\|^2 = \sum_n |\langle x, e_n \rangle|^2
$$

Every element has a **Fourier expansion**: $x = \sum_n \langle x, e_n \rangle e_n$.

**Theorem.** Every separable Hilbert space is isometrically isomorphic to $\ell^2$. This means that, up to isomorphism, there is essentially **one** separable infinite-dimensional Hilbert space.

## The Riesz Representation Theorem

**Theorem (Riesz–Fréchet).** Every bounded linear functional $f: H \to \mathbb{C}$ on a Hilbert space is of the form:

$$
f(x) = \langle x, y \rangle
$$

for a unique $y \in H$, and $\|f\| = \|y\|$.

This establishes a **conjugate-linear isometric isomorphism** $H^* \cong H$. It is one of the reasons Hilbert spaces are "self-dual" and so much nicer than general Banach spaces.

### Application: Lax–Milgram Theorem

**Theorem.** Let $a: H \times H \to \mathbb{R}$ be a continuous, coercive bilinear form (i.e., $a(u,u) \geq \alpha\|u\|^2$). Then for every $f \in H^*$, there exists a unique $u \in H$ with:

$$
a(u, v) = f(v) \quad \forall v \in H
$$

This is the foundation for the existence theory of elliptic PDEs in Sobolev spaces.

## Operators on Hilbert Spaces

### Self-Adjoint Operators

$T: H \to H$ is **self-adjoint** if $\langle Tx, y \rangle = \langle x, Ty \rangle$ for all $x, y$.

**Spectral Theorem (compact case).** If $T$ is a compact self-adjoint operator, then $H$ has an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors of $T$, and:

$$
Tx = \sum_n \lambda_n \langle x, e_n \rangle e_n
$$

where $\lambda_n \to 0$.

### Unitary Operators

$U: H \to H$ is **unitary** if $U^*U = UU^* = I$, equivalently $U$ is surjective and $\langle Ux, Uy \rangle = \langle x, y \rangle$.

In quantum mechanics, time evolution is given by a **one-parameter unitary group** $U(t) = e^{-iHt/\hbar}$ where $H$ is the Hamiltonian.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Hilbert spaces** are complete inner product spaces — the infinite-dimensional generalization of Euclidean geometry.
2. The **projection theorem** gives orthogonal decomposition: $H = M \oplus M^\perp$.
3. Separable Hilbert spaces are all isomorphic to $\ell^2$ via **orthonormal bases**.
4. The **Riesz representation theorem** makes $H^* \cong H$, enabling PDE theory via Lax–Milgram.
