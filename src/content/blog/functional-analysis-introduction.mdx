## Introduction

**Functional analysis** is the study of infinite-dimensional vector spaces equipped with a topology, typically arising from a norm or inner product. It provides the mathematical framework for quantum mechanics, partial differential equations, signal processing, and optimization. In this post, we introduce the central objects: Banach spaces and Hilbert spaces.

## Normed Spaces

A **normed space** is a vector space $V$ over $\mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{C}$) equipped with a function $\|\cdot\|: V \to [0, \infty)$ satisfying:

1. $\|x\| = 0 \iff x = 0$
2. $\|\alpha x\| = |\alpha| \|x\|$ for all scalars $\alpha$
3. $\|x + y\| \leq \|x\| + \|y\|$ (triangle inequality)

Every norm induces a metric: $d(x, y) = \|x - y\|$.

## Banach Spaces

A **Banach space** is a normed space that is **complete** — every Cauchy sequence converges.

### Key Examples

**1. The $\ell^p$ spaces** for $1 \leq p \leq \infty$:

$$
\ell^p = \left\{(x_n)_{n \geq 1} : \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |x_n|^p < \infty\right\}, \quad \|x\|_p = \left(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |x_n|^p\right)^{1/p}
$$

**2. The $L^p$ spaces:**

$$
L^p(\mu) = \left\{f : \int |f|^p \, d\mu < \infty\right\}, \quad \|f\|_p = \left(\int |f|^p \, d\mu\right)^{1/p}
$$

**3. $C([a,b])$** with the supremum norm $\|f\|_\infty = \sup_{x \in [a,b]} |f(x)|$.

## Hilbert Spaces

A **Hilbert space** is a Banach space whose norm comes from an inner product:

$$
\|x\| = \sqrt{\langle x, x \rangle}
$$

The inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ satisfies:
1. $\langle x, y \rangle = \overline{\langle y, x \rangle}$ (conjugate symmetry)
2. $\langle \alpha x + \beta y, z \rangle = \alpha\langle x, z\rangle + \beta\langle y, z\rangle$ (linearity)
3. $\langle x, x \rangle \geq 0$, with equality iff $x = 0$ (positive definiteness)

### The Parallelogram Law

A norm comes from an inner product if and only if it satisfies:

$$
\|x + y\|^2 + \|x - y\|^2 = 2\|x\|^2 + 2\|y\|^2
$$

This shows that $\ell^2$ and $L^2$ are Hilbert spaces, but $\ell^1$, $\ell^\infty$ are **not**.

### The Cauchy–Schwarz Inequality

$$
|\langle x, y \rangle| \leq \|x\| \cdot \|y\|
$$

with equality if and only if $x$ and $y$ are linearly dependent. This is arguably the most important inequality in all of mathematics.

## Orthogonality and Projections

Two vectors are **orthogonal** if $\langle x, y \rangle = 0$, written $x \perp y$.

**Projection Theorem.** Let $H$ be a Hilbert space and $M \subseteq H$ a closed subspace. Then every $x \in H$ has a unique decomposition:

$$
x = m + m^\perp, \quad m \in M, \quad m^\perp \in M^\perp
$$

where $m$ is the **best approximation** to $x$ from $M$:

$$
\|x - m\| = \inf_{y \in M} \|x - y\|
$$

## The Riesz Representation Theorem

**Theorem (Riesz).** Every continuous linear functional $\varphi: H \to \mathbb{C}$ on a Hilbert space can be uniquely represented as:

$$
\varphi(x) = \langle x, y \rangle
$$

for some $y \in H$, with $\|\varphi\| = \|y\|$.

This theorem establishes a canonical isomorphism $H \cong H^*$ — a Hilbert space is **self-dual**.

## Orthonormal Bases and Parseval's Identity

An **orthonormal basis** $\{e_n\}$ of $H$ satisfies $\langle e_m, e_n \rangle = \delta_{mn}$ and every $x \in H$ can be expanded:

$$
x = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \langle x, e_n \rangle \, e_n
$$

**Parseval's identity** then gives:

$$
\|x\|^2 = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |\langle x, e_n \rangle|^2
$$

This generalizes the Pythagorean theorem to infinite dimensions.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Banach spaces** = complete normed spaces; **Hilbert spaces** = complete inner product spaces.
2. The inner product gives Hilbert spaces **geometric structure** (angles, projections, orthogonality) that general Banach spaces lack.
3. The **Riesz representation theorem** and **projection theorem** are the pillars of Hilbert space theory.
4. Hilbert spaces are the natural home of **Fourier analysis** and **quantum mechanics**.
