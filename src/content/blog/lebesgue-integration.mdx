## Introduction

The **Lebesgue integral** is the correct generalization of the Riemann integral for modern analysis. Developed by Henri Lebesgue in his 1902 thesis, it integrates a much larger class of functions and has far superior convergence properties. Every analyst must understand why and how it works.

## The Limitation of Riemann

Recall that the Riemann integral partitions the **domain** $[a,b]$ into subintervals. This approach fails for highly irregular functions.

**Classic example:** The **Dirichlet function** $\chi_\mathbb{Q}$ on $[0,1]$:

$$
\chi_\mathbb{Q}(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & x \in \mathbb{Q} \\ 0 & x \notin \mathbb{Q} \end{cases}
$$

For any partition, every subinterval contains both rational and irrational numbers, so:

$$
\underline{\int} \chi_\mathbb{Q} = 0 \neq 1 = \overline{\int} \chi_\mathbb{Q}
$$

The Riemann integral does not exist. Yet intuitively, since the rationals have measure zero, the integral "should" be $0$.

## Lebesgue's Key Idea

Instead of partitioning the **domain**, Lebesgue partitions the **range**. Group together all points where $f$ takes similar values, then weight by the **measure** of each level set.

For a non-negative measurable function $f$:

$$
\int f \, d\mu = \sup\left\{\int s \, d\mu : 0 \leq s \leq f, \; s \text{ simple}\right\}
$$

where a **simple function** $s = \sum_{k=1}^n c_k \chi_{A_k}$ has integral $\int s \, d\mu = \sum_{k=1}^n c_k \, \mu(A_k)$.

For the Dirichlet function:

$$
\chi_\mathbb{Q} = 1 \cdot \chi_\mathbb{Q} + 0 \cdot \chi_{\mathbb{R}\setminus\mathbb{Q}}
$$

$$
\int_0^1 \chi_\mathbb{Q} \, d\lambda = 1 \cdot \lambda(\mathbb{Q} \cap [0,1]) + 0 \cdot \lambda((\mathbb{R}\setminus\mathbb{Q}) \cap [0,1]) = 1 \cdot 0 + 0 \cdot 1 = 0
$$

## The Three Great Convergence Theorems

These theorems are the real power of Lebesgue integration.

### 1. Monotone Convergence Theorem (MCT)

**Theorem (Beppo Levi).** If $0 \leq f_1 \leq f_2 \leq \cdots$ are measurable and $f_n \uparrow f$ pointwise, then:

$$
\int f \, d\mu = \lim_{n \to \infty} \int f_n \, d\mu
$$

We can **interchange the limit and the integral** for increasing sequences.

### 2. Fatou's Lemma

**Theorem.** For non-negative measurable functions $f_n$:

$$
\int \liminf_{n \to \infty} f_n \, d\mu \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} \int f_n \, d\mu
$$

The inequality can be strict (integration can "lose mass" in the limit).

### 3. Dominated Convergence Theorem (DCT)

**Theorem (Lebesgue).** If $f_n \to f$ pointwise a.e. and there exists $g \in L^1$ with $|f_n| \leq g$ a.e. for all $n$, then:

$$
\lim_{n \to \infty} \int f_n \, d\mu = \int f \, d\mu
$$

This is the most frequently used convergence theorem. The dominating function $g$ prevents mass from escaping to infinity.

## $L^p$ Spaces

The **Lebesgue spaces** are defined as:

$$
L^p(\mu) = \left\{f \text{ measurable} : \int |f|^p \, d\mu < \infty\right\}
$$

with norm $\|f\|_p = \left(\int |f|^p \, d\mu\right)^{1/p}$.

**Key results:**
- **Hölder's inequality:** $\|fg\|_1 \leq \|f\|_p \|g\|_q$ where $\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = 1$
- **Minkowski's inequality:** $\|f + g\|_p \leq \|f\|_p + \|g\|_p$
- **Riesz–Fischer theorem:** $L^p$ is **complete** (a Banach space)

## Comparison: Riemann vs. Lebesgue

| Property | Riemann | Lebesgue |
|---|---|---|
| Integrable functions | Continuous a.e. | Measurable + $L^1$ |
| Limit exchange | Very restrictive | MCT, DCT, Fatou |
| Completeness of $L^p$ | No | Yes |
| Works on general spaces | No | Yes ($\sigma$-finite) |
| $f$ Riemann $\implies$ Lebesgue | — | Yes, same value |

## Key Takeaways

1. Lebesgue's idea: partition the **range**, not the domain.
2. The **convergence theorems** (MCT, Fatou, DCT) are what make Lebesgue integration indispensable.
3. $L^p$ spaces, built on Lebesgue integration, are the **function spaces** of modern analysis.
4. Every Riemann integrable function is Lebesgue integrable with the same value — the Lebesgue integral is a strict generalization.
